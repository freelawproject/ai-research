<center><h1>OLSEN, SECRETARY OF LABOR OF NEBRASKA,<br>
v.<br>
NEBRASKA EX REL. WESTERN REFERENCE &amp; BOND ASSOCIATION, INC., ET AL.</h1></center>
<center>No. 671.</center>
<center><p><b>Supreme Court of United States.</b></p></center>
<center>Argued April 8, 9, 1941.</center>
<center>Decided April 28, 1941.</center>
CERTIORARI TO THE SUPREME COURT OF NEBRASKA.
<p><i>Mr. Don Kelley,</i> Assistant Attorney General of Nebraska, with whom <i>Mr. Walter R. Johnson,</i> Attorney General, was on the brief, for petitioner.</p>
<p><i>Mr. Walter Gordon Merritt</i> for respondents.</p>
<p><span class="star-pagination">*240</span> MR. JUSTICE DOUGLAS delivered the opinion of the Court.</p>
<p>In reliance upon <i>Ribnik</i> v. <i>McBride,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="101315"><a href="/opinion/101315/ribnik-v-mcbride/">277 U.S. 350</a></span>, the Supreme Court of Nebraska held, one judge dissenting, <span class="star-pagination">*241</span> that a statute of that state fixing the maximum compensation which a private employment agency might collect from an applicant for employment<sup>[1]</sup> was unconstitutional<sup>[2]</sup><span class="star-pagination">*242</span> under the due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. <span class="citation no-link">138 Neb. 574</span>, <span class="citation no-link">293 N.W. 393</span>. The case is here on a petition for certiorari which we granted because <span class="star-pagination">*243</span> of the importance of the constitutional question which was raised.</p>
<p>The action is for a peremptory writ of mandamus ordering petitioner, Secretary of Labor of Nebraska, to issue a license to the relator<sup>[3]</sup> to operate a private employment agency for the year commencing May 1, 1940. The license was withheld because of relator's refusal to limit its maximum compensation, as provided by the statute, to ten per cent of the first month's salary or wages of the person for whom employment was obtained. The petition in mandamus challenged the constitutionality of those provisions of the act.<sup>[4]</sup> The answer sought to sustain them by alleging that the business of a private employment agency is "vitally affected with a public interest" and subject to such regulation under the police power of the state. The relator's motion for judgment on the pleadings was sustained and it was ordered that a peremptory writ of mandamus should issue.</p>
<p>We disagree with the Supreme Court of Nebraska. The statutory provisions in question do not violate the due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.</p>
<p><span class="star-pagination">*244</span> The drift away from <i>Ribnik</i> v. <i>McBride, <span class="citation" data-id="101315"><a href="/opinion/101315/ribnik-v-mcbride/">supra</a></span></i><i>,</i> has been so great that it can no longer be deemed a controlling authority. It was decided in 1928. In the following year this Court held that Tennessee had no power to fix prices at which gasoline might be sold in the state. <i>Williams</i> v. <i>Standard Oil Co.,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="101356"><a href="/opinion/101356/williams-v-standard-oil-co-of-la/">278 U.S. 235</a></span>. Save for that decision and <i>Morehead</i> v. <i>Tipaldo,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102681"><a href="/opinion/102681/morehead-v-new-york-ex-rel-tipaldo/">298 U.S. 587</a></span>, holding unconstitutional a New York statute authorizing the fixing of women's wages, the subsequent cases in this Court have given increasingly wider scope to the price-fixing powers of the states and of Congress.<sup>[5]</sup><i>Tagg Bros.</i> v. <i>United States,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="101511"><a href="/opinion/101511/tagg-bros-moorhead-v-united-states/">280 U.S. 420</a></span>, decided in 1930, upheld the power of the Secretary of Agriculture under the Packers and Stockyards Act to determine the just and reasonable charges of persons engaged in the business of buying and selling in interstate commerce livestock at a stockyard on a commission basis. In 1931 a New Jersey statute limiting commissions of agents of fire insurance companies was sustained by <i>O'Gorman &amp; Young</i> v. <i>Hartford Fire Ins. Co.,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="101636"><a href="/opinion/101636/ogorman-young-inc-v-hartford-fire-ins-co/">282 U.S. 251</a></span>. A New York statute authorizing the fixing of minimum and maximum retail prices for milk was upheld in 1934. <i>Nebbia</i> v. <i>New York,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102224"><a href="/opinion/102224/nebbia-v-new-york/">291 U.S. 502</a></span>. And see <i>Hegeman Farms Corp.</i> v. <i>Baldwin,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102322"><a href="/opinion/102322/hegeman-farms-corp-v-baldwin/">293 U.S. 163</a></span>; <i>Borden's Farm Products Co.</i> v. <i>Ten Eyck,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102602"><a href="/opinion/102602/bordens-farm-products-co-inc-v-ten-eyck-comr-of-department-of/">297 U.S. 251</a></span>. Cf. <i>Baldwin</i> v. <i>G.A.F. Seelig, Inc.,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102402"><a href="/opinion/102402/baldwin-v-gaf-seelig-inc/">294 U.S. 511</a></span>; <i>Mayflower Farms</i> v. <i>Ten Eyck,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102603"><a href="/opinion/102603/mayflower-farms-inc-v-ten-eyck/">297 U.S. 266</a></span>. In 1937 <i>Adkins</i> v. <i>Children's Hospital,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="100181"><a href="/opinion/100181/adkins-v-childrens-hospital-of-dc/">261 U.S. 525</a></span>, was overruled and a statute of Washington which authorized the fixing of minimum wages for women and minors was sustained. <i>West Coast Hotel Co.</i> v. <i>Parrish,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102784"><a href="/opinion/102784/west-coast-hotel-co-v-parrish/">300 U.S. 379</a></span>. In the same year, <i>Townsend</i> v. <i>Yeomans.</i> <span class="citation" data-id="102835"><a href="/opinion/102835/townsend-v-yeomans/">301 U.S. 441</a></span>, upheld a <span class="star-pagination">*245</span> Georgia statute fixing maximum warehouse charges for the handling and selling of leaf tobacco. Cf. <i>Mulford</i> v. <i>Smith,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="103191"><a href="/opinion/103191/mulford-v-smith/">307 U.S. 38</a></span>; <i>Currin</i> v. <i>Wallace,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="103133"><a href="/opinion/103133/currin-v-wallace/">306 U.S. 1</a></span>. The power of Congress under the commerce clause to authorize the fixing of minimum prices for milk was upheld in <i>United States</i> v. <i>Rock Royal Co-operative,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="103227"><a href="/opinion/103227/united-states-v-rock-royal-co-op/">307 U.S. 533</a></span>, decided in 1939. The next year the price-fixing provisions of the Bituminous Coal Act of 1937 were sustained. <i>Sunshine Coal Co.</i> v. <i>Adkins,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="103362"><a href="/opinion/103362/sunshine-anthracite-coal-co-v-adkins/">310 U.S. 381</a></span>. And at this term we upheld the minimum wage and maximum hour provisions of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938. <i>United States</i> v. <i>Darby,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="103442"><a href="/opinion/103442/united-states-v-darby/">312 U.S. 100</a></span>. These cases represent more than scattered examples of constitutionally permissible price-fixing schemes. They represent in large measure a basic departure from the philosophy and approach of the majority in the <i>Ribnik</i> case. The standard there employed, following that used in <i>Tyson &amp; Brother</i> v. <i>Banton,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="101029"><a href="/opinion/101029/tyson-brother-v-banton/">273 U.S. 418</a></span>, 430 <i>et seq.,</i> was that the constitutional validity of price-fixing legislation, at least in absence of a so-called emergency,<sup>[6]</sup> was dependent on whether or not the business in question was "affected with a public interest." Cf. <i>Brazee</i> v. <i>Michigan,</i> <span class="citation" data-id="98749"><a href="/opinion/98749/brazee-v-michigan/">241 U.S. 340</a></span>. It was said to be so affected if it had been "devoted to the public use" and if "an interest in effect" had been granted "to the public in that use." <i>Ribnik</i> v. <i>McBride, supra,</i> p. 355. That test, labelled by Mr. Justice Holmes in his dissent in the <i>Tyson</i> case (273 U.S. at p. 446) as "little more than a fiction," was discarded in <i>Nebbia</i> v. <i>New York, supra,</i> pp. 531-539. It was there stated that such criteria "are not susceptible of definition and form an unsatisfactory test of the constitutionality of legislation directed at business practices or prices," and that the phrase "affected with a public interest" can mean "no more than <span class="star-pagination">*246</span> that an industry, for adequate reason, is subject to control for the public good." <i>Id.,</i> p. 536. And see the dissenting opinion in <i>Ribnik</i> v. <i>McBride, supra,</i> at p. 359.</p>
<p>The <i>Ribnik</i> case, freed from the test which it employed, can no longer survive. But respondents maintain that the statute here in question is invalid for other reasons. They insist that special circumstances must be shown to support the validity of such drastic legislation as price-fixing, that the executive, technical and professional workers which respondents serve have not been shown to be in need of special protection from exploitation, that legislative limitation of maximum fees for employment agencies is certain to react unfavorably upon those members of the community for whom it is most difficult to obtain jobs, that the increasing competition of public employment agencies and of charitable, labor union and employer association employment agencies have curbed excessive fees by private agencies, and that there is nothing in this record to overcome the presumption as to the result of the operation of such competitive, economic forces. And in the latter connection respondents urge that, since no circumstances are shown which curb competition between the private agencies and the other types of agencies, there are no conditions which the legislature might reasonably believe would redound to the public injury unless corrected by such legislation.</p>
<p>We are not concerned, however, with the wisdom, need, or appropriateness of the legislation. Differences of opinion on that score suggest a choice which "should be left where . . . it was left by the Constitution ย to the States and to Congress." <i>Ribnik</i> v. <i>McBride, supra,</i> at p. 375, dissenting opinion. There is no necessity for the state to demonstrate before us that evils persist despite the competition which attends the bargaining in this field. In final analysis, the only constitutional prohibitions <span class="star-pagination">*247</span> or restraints which respondents have suggested for the invalidation of this legislation are those notions of public policy embedded in earlier decisions of this Court but which, as Mr. Justice Holmes long admonished, should not be read into the Constitution. <i>Tyson &amp; Brother</i> v. <i>Banton, supra,</i> at p. 446; <i>Adkins</i> v. <i>Children's Hospital, supra,</i> at p. 570. Since they do not find expression in the Constitution, we cannot give them continuing vitality as standards by which the constitutionality of the economic and social programs of the states is to be determined.</p>
<p>The judgment is reversed and the cause is remanded to the Supreme Court of Nebraska for proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.</p>
<p><i>Reversed.</i></p>

A registration fee not to exceed two dollars may be charged by such licensed agency when such agency shall be at actual expense in advertising such individual applicant, or in looking up the reference of such applicant.
